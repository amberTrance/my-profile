A bit about me:

My name is Angela but I prefer Angie :). I have built this webpage in vanilla JavaScript, HTML and CSS. I am currently learning how to code and studying german. I like many different things and I've explored various fields throughout my life:

I've attended a school with an art profile throughout middle and high school. I love classical art, beautiful colours and painting. But I love other forms of art as well. During the same period, I've shortly attended a theatre course and vocal training. I've also had a short phase in which I wrote poetry.

At the end of highschool I've realized that I want to have a job that is intellectually stimulating. Philosophy was interesting to me, but I wanted something that is also applied. This is how I chose psychology. Studying the human mind remains for me one of the most interesting and challenging topics. However, I was not pleased with the current experimental approach and decided to leave the field.

If we leave the ethical aspects aside, scientific thinking and its discoveries are, in my opinion, the most beautiful achievements of humanity. I find it fascinating, for instance, that astronomers can tell the composition of distant stars and planets from the light wavelength received from those bodies. Or how a geologist can tell that a skeleton dates from these many million years ago because of the ground layer in which it finds itself. This is why I love watching various documentaries, and among my favourite are those of modern physics.

Coding contains in it many of the things I love. It always starts with a problem "How do I make this?". And to answer, you need to think creatively, logically, and also, to figure out the best implementation. Furthermore, you can perpetually learn new things, be surprised by what you can do, and take delight in getting better and better. This is how I came to conclude that being a software developer is probably the best job for me. In contrast, a neuroscientist career, which is what I have considered before, is perhaps 5% creative thinking, 45% reading other people's work, and the other 50% is a mixture of repetitive lab work, writing technical papers and pleading for funding, among other things. So, coding it is!! :D
